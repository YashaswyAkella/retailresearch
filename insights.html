The research in plain language

1. Joint sales follow an inverted U

When departments are too close, shoppers categorize them as substitutes. When they're too far, they feel unrelated. The sweet spot — where joint purchases peak — is related-but-distinct. The paper's inflection is at 23.76 m.

"Close departments are perceived to be substitutes, decreasing the likelihood of buying products from both departments. As distance increases, departments are perceived as somewhat related but different, increasing their diversity and the likelihood of buying from both."

2. Mix your layouts on purpose

Using different layouts across departments (grid + free-flow + racetrack) amplifies the IDD effect. Breaking shopping scripts forces shoppers to re-engage with categorization.

3. Variety differentials = free attention

When two departments differ a lot in how many categories they offer (e.g., 2-cat confectionery next to 8-cat baking), shopper awareness spikes. Bigger differentials → stronger IDD effect on joint sales.

4. Practical rules for store managers

  • Substitutes (fresh meat & processed meat) → place close, enable comparison.
  • Related-but-distinct (bread & cheese, cuisine & baking) → place at moderate distance.
  • Unrelated (babies, household chemicals, dairy) → spread out to remind shoppers of forgotten needs.
  • Add inspirational signage (recipes) between far-apart but related departments.

5. The novel instrument — Theta (θ)

To deal with endogeneity, the authors used the angle θ at the store entrance between department midpoints. The entrance is usually fixed by parking, making θ a clean geometric proxy for distance.
